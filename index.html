The Human Eye

Structure & Function

  • Cornea: Transparent dome, major refraction, protection.
  • Iris: Colored diaphragm, controls pupil size for light entry.
  • Pupil: Opening in iris, diameter changes with light.
  • Ciliary Muscles: Adjust lens curvature for focus (accommodation).
  • Lens: Flexible, biconvex, focuses image on retina.
  • Retina: Light-sensitive, rods (dim/night), cones (color/day).
  • Optic Nerve: Relays visual info to brain.

How Eye Works

  1. Light enters cornea, is refracted.
  2. Passes through aqueous humour and pupil.
  3. Lens focuses by changing shape (ciliary muscle).
  4. Forms image on retina.
  5. Retina’s rods/cones convert image; optic nerve sends to brain.
  6. Brain perceives the upright, colored, real image.

Accommodation & Persistence

  • Lens thickness changes for near/far objects (see diagram; drag to see).
  • Least distance of distinct vision: 25 cm (adult normal eye).
  • Persistence of vision: Image lingers 1/16 s; enables movies.

  • Activity: Why do tube lights look steady? (Persistence merges flickers.)
  • Numerical: Near point 100cm, wants 25cm: P≈-3D (solution above).

Eye Defects & Correction

Myopia (Short Sightedness)

  • Cause: Eyeball too long or lens too curved; image in front of retina, distant objects blurry.
  • Correction: Concave lens diverges rays, focuses on retina. Ray diagram below:

Hypermetropia (Long Sight)

  • Cause: Eyeball too short/lens too weak; image behind retina, near objects blurry.
  • Correction: Convex lens converges rays onto retina. Animated diagram below:

Presbyopia

  • Age: Ciliary muscles weaken, lens rigid.
  • Correction: Bifocal lenses for near & far vision.

Prism • Dispersion • Rainbow

Refraction by Prism

  • Prism refracts light twice (entry & exit face).
  • Monochromatic: Bends only.
  • White light: Splits (dispersion)—VIBGYOR, violet bends most, red least.

Rainbow Formation

  1. Sunlight enters drop – refracts, disperses.
  2. Reflects inside drop.
  3. Exits, refracts again—observer sees spectrum.

Q&A

  • Why is red on top/violet at bottom in a rainbow? Different departure angles on exit from drop.
  • Draw labelled diagram: prism dispersion and rainbow formation. (See animation above.)

In-text Activities / Reasoning

  • Why do we see a rainbow only when the Sun is behind us and rain ahead? Proper angle for dispersed light to enter eye.
  • Activity: Use CD/droplets to see mini rainbows at home.

Atmospheric Refraction

Twinkling of Stars

  • Layers of air with varying temperature—random bends of starlight shift its image, causing twinkle.
  • Planets don’t twinkle: They appear as discs—light averages out.

Apparent Sunrise/Sunset

  • Due to refraction, Sun seen before actually above horizon (sunrise), after actually set (sunset).
  • Sun appears flattened—lower part’s light refracted more.

Q&A

  • Explain with diagram: advanced sunrise/delayed sunset.
  • Why does Sun appear elliptical at times? Lower limb’s light bent more than upper.

Exam Q's

  • Why do we see the Sun even when it is still below the horizon?
  • Why only stars twinkle?

Sky Colour & Tyndall Effect

Why is the Sky Blue?

  • Blue (shorter) wavelengths scattered more by air molecules (Rayleigh effect).
  • Sunset/Sunrise: Sunlight passes longer through atmosphere; blue/red scattered most/least.
  • Clouds white: Water droplets scatter all colors nearly equally.

Tyndall Effect

  • Scattering by particles (dust, smoke, water droplets)—beam of light visible in dust.
  • Experiment: Shine torch in chalk-solution, see bluish column.

Q&A

  • Why does the Sun appear red at sunrise/sunset? Blue is scattered; only red/orange light reaches observer.
